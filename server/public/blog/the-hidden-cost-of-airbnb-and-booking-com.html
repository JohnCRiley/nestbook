The hidden cost of Airbnb and Booking.com — what the commission really means over a season

NB
The NestBook Team
April 2026
· 4 min read

Nobody talks about this openly. Property owners list on the platforms, accept the commission as a cost of doing business, and quietly absorb the loss. But when you actually do the maths, the numbers are surprising.

What the platforms charge

Airbnb charges hosts between 3% and 16% per booking depending on the listing type and cancellation policy. Booking.com typically charges between 15% and 18%. These percentages apply to the total booking value including cleaning fees.

For a modest gîte charging €800 per week, that commission is between €120 and €144 per booking — before you've changed a single towel.

What a season actually costs

Let's take a realistic example. A four-bedroom gîte in the south of France, running from April to October. Twenty bookings over the season at an average of €900 per week.

Total revenue: €18,000

Platform commission at 15%: €2,700

Platform commission at 18%: €3,240

That's between €2,700 and €3,240 leaving your account every season — in commission alone. Over five years, that's between €13,500 and €16,200.

For a UK B&B taking £150 per night with an average stay of three nights, twenty bookings a season generates £9,000. At 15% commission that's £1,350 per season — £6,750 over five years.

The platform isn't just taking a cut. It's taking a significant portion of what could be your profit margin.

Why owners accept it

The platforms offer something genuinely valuable: visibility. Millions of travellers search Airbnb and Booking.com every day. For a new property with no reputation and no website, getting listed is the fastest way to get those first bookings.

The commission makes sense as a marketing cost when you're starting out.

The mistake is treating it as a permanent cost of doing business rather than a temporary bridge to building your own direct booking channel.

The direct booking alternative

A guest who books directly through your website costs you nothing in commission. You need a simple booking widget on your site — something guests can use to check availability, pick their dates and confirm their stay without going through a third party.

The widget pays for itself on the first booking.

Most experienced property owners use a hybrid approach: the platforms for new guests and visibility, direct bookings for returning guests and personal referrals. Over time, as your reputation builds and your direct booking channel grows, the balance shifts.

The goal isn't to leave the platforms entirely. It's to make sure every guest who loved their stay knows they can book direct next time — and that booking direct is easy.

A simple calculation

If just four of your twenty annual bookings become direct bookings, at £150 per night for three nights, you save £270 in commission.

That's more than enough to cover a year of simple booking management software. The rest is profit.
